How many times have you heard that you're smart but lazy?

I heard it so many times that I started believing it. Teachers said it. Managers said it. I said it to myself.

The pattern was always the same: I'd start strong, create something interesting, then lose focus and abandon it. Later, someone would ask why I didn't finish, and the only answer I had was "I'm lazy."

Except I wasn't lazy. I was ADHD. And those look very different when you understand what's happening under the hood.

ADHD isn't about motivation. It's about regulation. It's not that I don't want to do something — it's that my brain's executive function has gaps that make sustained focus feel like pushing a boulder uphill while everyone else walks on flat ground.

The worst part? Everyone was right. And everyone was wrong. I AM smart. I AM driven. But I'm also struggling with something that doesn't have a label people understand, so they fit me into the nearest box: lazy.

The unlock came when I stopped fighting the label and started working with the neurology. Timers instead of willpower. Structure instead of self-discipline. Medication that actually addresses the root cause instead of supplemental coffee that addresses the symptom.

The work that used to take weeks to start and months to fail through now actually gets finished.

If you've heard "smart but lazy" enough times to believe it — maybe it's worth checking if there's something else going on. Something with a name. Something treatable.

You might not be lazy. You might just be wired differently.
