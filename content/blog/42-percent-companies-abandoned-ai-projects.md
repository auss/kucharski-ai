S&P Global's 2025 survey shows AI project abandonment jumped from 17% to 42% compared to the previous year. That's not hype fading — something is systematically breaking.

The pattern is the same everywhere. A team gets AI tools and productivity spikes immediately. Then the first bugs appear, someone asks AI to fix them, and the fix breaks something else. Each iteration makes things worse until the project dies or gets scrapped entirely.

I've seen this loop in my own work. I thought I was the problem — bad prompts, wrong approach, not enough context. I wasn't failing, I was just early to a pattern that's now hitting organizations at scale.

The root cause isn't complexity or missing skills. It's that AI made generating code cheaper than understanding it — when you can create faster than you can comprehend, every fix adds chaos instead of clarity.

We keep treating this as an adoption problem: more licenses, more training, more use cases. But the 42% aren't failing because they lack tools, they're failing because nobody taught teams when to stop generating and start understanding.

Here's a test: before the next AI fix, ask your team to explain what that code does — without AI. If they can't, don't let AI touch it. You'll compound the confusion, not solve it.

How many projects in your organization would fail that test right now?
